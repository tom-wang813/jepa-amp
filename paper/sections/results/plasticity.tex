Frozen ESM-2 embeddings are more directly useful: they achieve higher $k$-NN
MIC correlation, linear probe AUROC, and silhouette score without any
fine-tuning.
However, the pattern \emph{reverses} after supervised fine-tuning: JEPA-AMP
outperforms ESM-2 by $+0.086$ Pearson on GRAMPA MIC (Table~\ref{tab:mic}).
This reversal suggests that JEPA's latent-space prediction objective creates
representations that are less immediately discriminative but more
\emph{adaptable}: they have greater headroom for task-specific fine-tuning,
whereas ESM-2's masked-token objective produces representations that are
already near their downstream utility ceiling.

UMAP visualisations of both embedding spaces are provided in
Figure~\ref{fig:umap}.
In JEPA space, AMP sequences form peripheral satellite clusters distinct from
non-AMP, while in ESM-2 space the separation is more centripetal.
Generated peptides occupy a distinct region of the JEPA embedding space
from the training corpus, consistent with the 100\% exact-match novelty score
and a median nearest-neighbour identity of 0.526.

\finding{Frozen JEPA embeddings are less discriminative than frozen ESM-2, but more
adaptable under supervised fine-tuning. This supports a ``plasticity''
interpretation: JEPA pre-training preserves representational capacity that
is unlocked by task-specific heads, rather than committing early to
discriminative boundaries.}
