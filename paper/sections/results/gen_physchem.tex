\subsection{Physicochemical-Conditioned Generation}
\label{sec:res_gen}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Physicochemical generation-control ablation.
  All variants share the frozen JEPA encoder.
  Values are charge $R^2$ and MAE over 2{,}000 generated sequences across
  10 target conditions (\texttt{eval\_results/generation\_control\_formal}).}
\label{tab:gen_ablation}
\resizebox{\textwidth}{!}{%
\begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Variant} & \textbf{Charge $R^2$} & \textbf{Charge MAE}
  & \textbf{GRAVY $R^2$} & \textbf{Length $R^2$} & \textbf{Unique frac.} \\
\midrule
Weak-cond.\ baseline (v2, cross-attn token only) & $-11.83$ & 5.73 & $-1.02$ & $-1.91$ & 0.995 \\
AdaLN-only baseline (v3)                          & $-17.90$ & 5.53 & $-0.83$ & $-9.54$ & 0.965 \\
\midrule
Dual-pathway (v4, proposed)                        & $\mathbf{0.866}$ & $\mathbf{2.23}$ & 0.020 & $-1.71$ & 0.946 \\

\end{table}

The proposed dual-pathway decoder achieves charge $R^2{=}\mathbf{0.866}$ and
MAE~\textbf{2.23}, while both baselines have negative charge $R^2$ and are
condition-invariant.
GRAVY and length remain weakly controlled (GRAVY $R^2{=}0.020$, length
$R^2{=}{-}1.71$), indicating that direct charge control does not generalise to
other physicochemical axes.
The ablation runs show that both design choices matter.
Removing the auxiliary loss breaks charge control completely
($R^2{=}{-}16.763$), while removing CFG dropout lowers charge control
($R^2{=}0.805$) and also lowers uniqueness (0.836 vs.\ 0.946).

Figure~\ref{fig:charge_sweep} shows continuous charge sweeps ($q$ from $-9$ to
$+13$) with fixed context and GRAVY; the generated charge tracks the target
smoothly across the full range.

\finding{The dual-pathway decoder reliably controls net charge, while GRAVY and length
remain property-specific failures. Both conditioning baselines collapse to
condition-invariant output, demonstrating that neither cross-attention tokens
alone nor AdaLN alone suffice.}
