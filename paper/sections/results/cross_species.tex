\subsection{Cross-Species Zero-Shot Transfer}
\label{sec:res_cross_species}

To probe whether JEPA-AMP learns transferable MIC representations rather than
species-specific patterns, we evaluate zero-shot cross-species generalisation:
a model trained on a \emph{source} bacterial species is applied without
re-training to a held-out \emph{target} species.
We test all five directed routes spanning E.\,coli, S.\,aureus, and
P.\,aeruginosa (three seeds, $n_{\text{test}}$ ranging from 402 to 796 per
route).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Zero-shot cross-species MIC prediction (Pearson $r$, mean $\pm$ SD
  over seeds 42, 7, 123).
  In-domain results (trained on target species) are shown for reference.
  Statistical comparison uses paired permutation testing across all 15
  route--seed observations.}
\label{tab:cross_species}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
\textbf{Route} & \textbf{JEPA zero-shot} & \textbf{ESM-2 zero-shot} & \textbf{JEPA in-domain} \\
\midrule
E.~coli $\to$ S.~aureus       & $0.553 \pm 0.040$ & $0.451 \pm 0.030$ & $0.598 \pm 0.039$ \\
E.~coli $\to$ P.~aeruginosa   & $0.657 \pm 0.020$ & $0.547 \pm 0.046$ & $0.603 \pm 0.013$ \\
S.~aureus $\to$ E.~coli       & $0.384 \pm 0.055$ & $0.300 \pm 0.036$ & $0.628 \pm 0.038$ \\
S.~aureus $\to$ P.~aeruginosa & $0.484 \pm 0.028$ & $0.403 \pm 0.030$ & $0.620 \pm 0.021$ \\
P.~aeruginosa $\to$ E.~coli   & $0.536 \pm 0.022$ & $0.417 \pm 0.044$ & $0.582 \pm 0.026$ \\
\midrule
\textbf{Mean}                 & $\mathbf{0.523}$ & $0.423$ & $0.606$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

Across all five routes JEPA-AMP outperforms the ESM-2 baseline in zero-shot
transfer (mean $\Delta = +0.100$, 95\,\% CI $[0.073, 0.126]$,
permutation $p < 0.001$, $n = 15$ paired observations).
The gain is consistent in direction across every route, with the largest
advantage on the P.\,aeruginosa $\to$ E.\,coli route
($\Delta = +0.119$) and the E.\,coli $\to$ P.\,aeruginosa route
($\Delta = +0.110$).

Notably, on E.\,coli $\to$ P.\,aeruginosa JEPA zero-shot ($r = 0.657$)
\emph{matches} JEPA in-domain ($r = 0.603$), suggesting the E.\,coli
representation generalises particularly well to this gram-negative target.
The harder gram-positive transfer (S.\,aureus $\to$ E.\,coli, $r = 0.384$)
leaves a larger gap versus in-domain, indicating that membrane-composition
differences remain a challenge.

\finding{JEPA-AMP representations transfer across bacterial species with no
re-training, outperforming the ESM-2 baseline by $\Delta r = +0.100$ on
average ($p < 0.001$).
Domain-specific fine-tuning closes a substantial additional gap, pointing
to few-shot adaptation as a practical deployment pathway.}
